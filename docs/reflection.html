What worked well during the CCL?

The first thing I really enjoyed was, of course, the design phase. Having the freedom to decide on the topic and visual direction made me feel deeply engaged with the project.

Seeing the design and prototype come together was the first big moment of success — honestly, who doesn’t love it when a prototype is finished and works exactly as intended?

During the coding phase, almost every step felt like a small success. Since I still struggle with coding at times, every working feature felt meaningful. Due to a private family situation, I had to pause for almost a week, which made coming back and pushing through even more rewarding.

From design to frontend, backend, and finally hosting (which was a struggle — not gonna lie), I genuinely feel proud of having completed the project.

Biggest challenges and struggles

My biggest struggles were, once again, typos and Git best practices. The typos mostly happen because I tend to get a bit too excited and move too fast — which is annoying, but also kind of funny in hindsight. Most of the time, the issue really was just a typo.

Git was another challenge. Because I often worked at different times and places, I frequently forgot to commit. This became especially frustrating when trying new things that didn’t work out, forcing me to manually undo changes instead of simply rolling back.

Mea culpa.

What I managed to understand better

Thanks to CCL and the freedom to choose my own topic, I became much more invested in making the design work properly. I spent a lot of time digging into CSS and HTML. While the result isn’t perfect, it helped me understand layout, structure, and styling much better.

Another important learning was staying calm when things didn’t work. I learned that most errors were not catastrophic — they were usually just small mistakes. This mindset made debugging much easier.

I also finally got a solid grasp of backend and database concepts. While I had learned the theory before, applying it to a personal project and adapting it to my own ideas made everything click.

In the end, even though the result might not be exactly how I imagined it at the start, I built it, hosted it, and I stand behind it. That alone makes me very proud.

What I still struggle with

Hosting is still something I struggle with — just like last year. Even though the steps are clearly documented, it still takes me much longer than I’d like to deploy a project.

Another ongoing challenge is staying focused. Small, careless mistakes sometimes lead to larger errors. However, I’ve definitely improved at debugging and error handling, and it no longer takes me half a day to find simple issues.

Looking back at the start of the semester

Looking back, I did believe I would be able to build the app I delivered — not necessarily because I had full confidence in my skills, but because I knew from the previous semester that I had managed to make things work, even when I initially thought I wouldn’t.
